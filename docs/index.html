Clean Air and Green Space Follow Wealth in Swiss Cities

While national statistics show contradictory pollution trends, zooming into cities reveals a systematic pattern. A data investigation into how Swiss cities quietly segregate by environmental quality.

By Olaf König

August 2025

Environmental inequalities—the unequal distribution of pollution, noise, and green space across social groups—reveal how wealth shapes not just economic opportunity, but the very air we breathe and spaces we inhabit.

At the national scale, Switzerland's pollution geography paints a paradoxical picture. While the wealthiest escape noise pollution—affecting roughly one wealthy household in five versus one poor household in three—they breathe more polluted air, with nearly one wealthy household in five exceeding nitrogen dioxide thresholds compared to one poor household in seven.

But these national averages mask dramatic variations within individual cities. Our investigation focused on Switzerland's ten largest urban centers, where local dynamics create very different environmental realities.

How do these local dynamics actually unfold? What happens when we examine each city's internal geography, where wealth and environmental quality intersect? Let's explore three contrasting examples.

The Privilege Pattern

Stack the data from all ten cities, and privilege writes itself in stark ratios. Noise pollution strikes 1 wealthy household in 5, but 1 poor family in 2. Air pollution exceeding WHO guidelines affects 1 wealthy household in 2—and nearly 3 poor families in 4.

The higher your social status, the more likely you are to escape noise, pollution, and concrete.

City by City Variations

But not all Swiss cities are equally unequal. The environmental divide varies dramatically from one urban area to another.

Some cities show consistent inequality across all indicators. Winterthur maintains a steady 30-point gap whether measuring noise, air, or green access. Others specialize. Bern achieves remarkable equality for noise and green space, yet uniquely reverses the pattern for air pollution, with wealthy households more exposed than poor ones. And Lucerne stands out as the green equality champion, with only a 7-point gap for nature access.

From St. Gallen's stark divides to Geneva's relative equality, Switzerland's cities reveal a universal urban truth. Social privilege and environmental privilege walk hand in hand. The question now is whether this quiet segregation will remain invisible—or finally demand attention.

Methodology ▼

Data Sources and Study Area

This analysis examines environmental inequalities across Switzerland's 10 largest cities by population in 2023. The study combines household-level socio-economic data originally from the Swiss Federal Statistical Office (SFSO) but updated and processed according to Panczak et al. (2023) with multiple environmental exposure datasets to assess disparities in environmental quality across socio-economic groups (reflecting 2015 conditions).

Socio-economic status was measured using the Swiss-SEP 3 (Swiss neighbourhood index of socioeconomic position 3), a hybrid index that combines updated values for neighborhoods around buildings constructed after 2000 (based on 2012-2015 micro-censuses) with original Swiss-SEP 1 values for older neighborhoods (based on 2000 census data). This high-resolution index defines neighborhoods around each residential building comprising approximately 50 households, incorporating variables such as rent per square meter, overcrowding, education level, and occupational classification.

Environmental exposures were assessed through three key indicators:

  • Daytime road traffic noise: 10-meter resolution raster released by FOEN in 2025 (reflecting 2021 conditions)
  • Nitrogen dioxide concentrations: 20-meter resolution raster produced by Meteotest in 2021
  • Green space access: derived from satellite imagery using the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) from Copernicus satellite data processed through Google Earth Engine, with values averaged across summer months (June to August) for the period 2022-2024 (König, 2024)

Spatial extraction between household points and environmental rasters was performed using bilinear interpolation method.

Due to statistical confidentiality requirements for household-level data, results are presented in generalized form using hexagonal grids and spatial clusters in maps.

Analytical Framework

The analysis employed a local quartile classification approach, dividing households within each city into four socio-economic groups based on their SSEP scores, where quartile 1 represents the lowest socio-economic status and quartile 4 the highest. Similarly, environmental exposures were classified into local quartiles for each city, with quartile 1 indicating the lowest exposure or best environmental conditions, and quartile 4 representing the highest exposure or worst conditions.

Environmental inequalities were quantified by calculating the percentage of households in each socio-economic quartile that exceeded established environmental health thresholds. For noise pollution, the threshold was set at 50 dB. Air pollution exposure was measured against NO₂ concentrations of 15 μg/m³. Green space access was assessed by identifying households with "limited" vegetation coverage, defined as less than 50% "green surface" (corresponding to NDVI values below 0.5) within a 200-meter radius.

The magnitude of environmental inequality within each city was calculated as the percentage point difference in exposure rates between the lowest and highest socio-economic quartiles. Negative values indicate disproportionately higher exposure among lower socio-economic groups, representing environmental injustice patterns.

Methodological Considerations

The NDVI indicator was inverted to represent "lack of green access" rather than green abundance, ensuring consistency with pollution exposure metrics where higher values indicate worse environmental conditions.

This local quartile classification approach enables city-specific analysis while maintaining comparability across different urban contexts, accounting for the varying baseline environmental conditions and socio-economic distributions across Swiss cities. The methodology combines official administrative data with satellite-derived environmental indicators, providing a comprehensive assessment of environmental exposure patterns at high spatial resolution.

Other Data Sources

  • Water bodies: swissTLM3D from swisstopo for lake boundaries (to exclude water surfaces from NDVI calculations for waterfront residents)
